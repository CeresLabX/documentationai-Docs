## Etymology

Sandalore takes its name from its sandalwood-like character: the prefix "Sandal-" refers directly to sandalwood, while the suffix "-ore" is a stylistic ending that suggests preciousness and odor value rather than strict chemical nomenclature.

The IUPAC name of the main isomer is `3-methyl-5-(2,2,3-trimethylcyclopent-3-en-1-yl)pentan-2-ol`, and it is sometimes described more generically as a sandalwood pentanol derivative.

After patent expiry, Sandalore has also appeared under alternative trade names, including:

- Dersantol
- Sandela
- Sandasweet
- Sandal touch

These materials are all sandalwood-type aroma chemicals closely related to or equivalent to Sandalore, depending on supplier and specification.

## Olfactory profile

Sandalore is a creamy, milky, velvety sweet-woody sandalwood note with strong diffusion and high substantivity.

It provides a modern, clean sandalwood impression rather than the complex, slightly animalic facets of natural Santalum album oil, and tends to behave in a relatively linear way over the life of a perfume.

### Structured organoleptic evaluation

Sensory evaluation of Sandalore across key facets:

- **Woody**: 9 / 10, **very long** duration
- **Creamy**: 8 / 10, **long** duration
- **Balsamic**: 6 / 10, **medium** duration
- **Musky**: 5 / 10, **long** duration

Additional analytical and sensory data:

- **Tenacity**: &gt;400 hours on a smelling blotter under standard test conditions
- **Odor threshold (air)**: approximately **0.7 ppt**
- **Odor threshold (mass in air)**: around **2.996 ng/L**
- **Volatility**: **very low**; vapor pressure around **0.001 hPa (0.247 Pa) at 25°C**, roughly **1,000 times less volatile than water**

This combination explains why Sandalore provides a persistent sandalwood aura rather than a fleeting top note burst.

### Odor character

Key descriptors:

- Creamy and milky texture
- Smooth, velvety sandalwood woodiness
- Sweet, slightly lactonic facet
- Clean, modern sandalwood tone rather than smoky or incense-like notes

The evolution on skin and blotter is relatively linear, with the sandalwood character maintained from top through the drydown.

### Diffusion, volatility, and the intensity paradox

Sandalore shows a strong, diffusive top note despite being a base note, and then settles into a long-lasting, stable drydown.

Typical temporal perception:

- **Top**: first 5 to 15 minutes — bright, diffusive sandalwood with creamy sweetness
- **Heart**: around 2 to 6 hours — smoother, slightly softer sandalwood, still clearly present
- **Drydown**: &gt;8 hours — persistent sandalwood base with a milky, woody aura

Analytical parameters such as odor threshold and vapor pressure suggest a very potent material, yet many evaluators experience an **intensity paradox**: Sandalore is often perceived as relatively subtle or even "transparent". This appears linked to its large molecular size, which can cause **specific anosmia** in some individuals and **rapid olfactory fatigue** in others, so the nose adapts quickly even while the material remains present at effective levels.

Substantivity testing on smelling strips indicates:

- **Tenacity on strip**: &gt;400 hours
- **Perception in practice**: sandalwood effect can be noticeable for more than 2 weeks on a well-sprayed blotter stored properly

<Callout kind="info">

If Sandalore suddenly seems to vanish while you are smelling it, you may be experiencing specific anosmia or short-term olfactory fatigue. Rest your nose, revisit later, and compare with other evaluators before changing your formula.

</Callout>

### Structure and scent correlation

Sandalore conforms to the classic **sandalwood olfactophore model**, which links molecular features to the characteristic sandalwood odor.

Key structural features and their sensory consequences:

- A **bulky, lipophilic group** based on a **cyclopentene ring** provides the hydrophobic mass needed for a creamy, woody impression and helps anchor the molecule at the receptor site.
- A **hydroxyl group (`-OH`)** forms a **tertiary alcohol osmophore**, which is critical to sandalwood character and contributes to the soft, milky warmth.
- **Low volatility** from the overall size and lipophilicity of the molecule aligns with its role as a base note and explains its very long substantivity.
- Sandalore is a **mixture of stereoisomers**; the three-dimensional fit of these isomers into **sandalwood-sensitive olfactory receptors** is believed to fine-tune its woody, creamy profile and may underpin subtle differences between suppliers and batches.

Understanding this structure–odor relationship helps when comparing Sandalore with other synthetic sandalwood materials or when designing accords that emphasize its creamy warmth versus its woody backbone.

### Related sandalwood chemicals

Sandalore sits in a family of synthetic sandalwood odorants often used together or in alternation to build a rich sandalwood accord.

Common related materials include:

- **Ebanol**
- **Javanol**
- **Polysantol**
- **Bacdanol / Sanjanol**

These differ in impact, creaminess, and naturalness, but they all occupy the same woody sandalwood space and can be combined to fine-tune texture and diffusion.

## History and background

Sandalore was developed by Givaudan in the 1970s, at a time when natural Santalum album (Mysore sandalwood) oil was becoming increasingly scarce and expensive.

A United States patent covering Sandalore was granted in 1976, reflecting its importance as an early modern sandalwood synthetic.

From the late 1980s onward, Sandalore became a key component of the "big sandalwood" style of perfumery:

- It contributed significantly to the character of **Guerlain Samsara (1989)**, which helped define a generation of sandalwood-focused orientals.
- It was also important in **Chanel Égoïste (1990)**, where its creamy woodiness underpins the fragrance's distinctive personality and supports the perfume's iconic marketing and advertising campaigns.

In the 2010s, Sandalore gained additional attention outside perfumery.

Academic research showed that:

- Skin cells express an olfactory receptor called **OR2AT4** that responds to Sandalore, and activation of this receptor can support **wound-healing processes** in vitro (reported in 2014).
- In 2018, further work indicated that Sandalore can influence **hair follicle biology**, promoting anagen (growth) phase markers in hair follicles under laboratory conditions.

These findings do not change its regulatory status as a fragrance ingredient, but they have increased scientific and popular interest in Sandalore as an example of extra-nasal olfactory signaling.

## Source and production

Sandalore is a fully synthetic aroma chemical; there is no natural source material.

A commonly described industrial route uses **campholenic aldehyde** (itself derived from **alpha-pinene**) as the starting material.

A simplified production outline is:

- Derive campholenic aldehyde from alpha-pinene.
- Perform an **aldol condensation** with butan-2-one to build the side chain.
- Apply **selective hydrogenation** to obtain the desired alcohol.
- Purify the resulting Sandalore fraction by **vacuum distillation** to achieve perfumery-grade quality.

Sustainability and environmental data from suppliers indicate:

- Sandalore contains **more than 50% renewable carbon** based on the biobased alpha-pinene feedstock (per Givaudan data).
- It is considered **inherently biodegradable** according to OECD test methods.

### Industrial production and regional producers

Commercial production is now carried out by multiple global aroma chemical manufacturers, and the material is widely available as a commodity sandalwood ingredient.

Named examples include:

- **Givaudan** (Switzerland), the original developer and a major ongoing producer
- **DRT** (France), now part of **Firmenich**, producing sandalwood-type ingredients on an industrial scale
- Various large fragrance and aroma chemical firms in **China** and **India**, supplying the broader market with Sandalore-type materials

### Commercial grades and suppliers

Commercial Sandalore is usually supplied as a **neat, viscous liquid**, often at **greater than 95% purity** for perfumery use.

Key points:

- Major fragrance houses and specialty chemical companies distribute Sandalore in bulk.
- Large chemical distributors offer it as a standard sandalwood ingredient for industrial customers.
- Specifications typically include purity, isomer ratio ranges, color, and odor profile checks.

## Physical and chemical properties

Key physical and chemical data for Sandalore:

- **Molecular weight**: 210.36 grams per mole (g/mol)
- **Molecular formula**: C14H26O
- **Boiling point**: approximately 275°C
- **Flash point**: greater than 95°C
- **Appearance**: colorless to pale yellow, viscous liquid

Solubility and compatibility:

- **Water solubility**: low, around 17 milligrams per liter (mg/L)
- **Soluble in**:
  - Ethanol
  - Dipropylene glycol (DPG)
  - Common perfumery solvents such as isopropyl myristate (IPM) and other organic solvents

These properties make Sandalore straightforward to handle and dose in alcohol-based fine fragrances, functional products, and oil-based bases.

## Use in perfumery

Sandalore is a versatile sandalwood building block used as:

- A **signature sandalwood note** in woody and oriental compositions
- A **base-note enhancer** that adds diffusion and creamy volume to sandalwood accords
- A **fixative and harmonizer** that smooths blends and extends the life of floral, woody, and musky accords

Typical fragrance families where Sandalore excels include:

- Woody and woody-amber fragrances
- Orientals and amber orientals
- Floral compositions (especially jasmine, rose, and muguet themes)
- Chypres and neo-chypres
- Fougeres, where it can enrich the base beneath coumarin, lavender, and moss-like notes

Blending suggestions:

- **Florals**: jasmine, rose, and muguet accords benefit from Sandalore's creamy sandalwood cushion.
- **Woods**: blends well with cedarwood derivatives to create structured, modern woody bases.
- **Spices and vanilla**: adds warmth and depth, smoothing harsher spice edges.
- **Musks**: rounds off musky bases, giving them a more sophisticated, creamy woody tail.
- **Other sandalwood synthetics**: layer with materials like Ebanol, Javanol, or Polysantol to fine-tune naturalness vs. radiance.

### Dosage guidelines

Work within these broad ranges, adjusting for style, regulators, and personal taste:

- **Modern use**: around **0.1% to 10% of the concentrate** in most fine fragrance applications.
- **Historical high use**: up to **12% of the concentrate** (for example, Tom Ford Amber Absolute, 2007).

Because Sandalore is a base note with strong substantivity, even low dosages (below 1% in concentrate) may have a noticeable effect over time.

<Callout kind="info">

Assess Sandalore in a **10% ethanol dilution** on blotter and skin to understand its impact across top, heart, and drydown. Its apparent linearity and subtlety at low doses can make it easy to underestimate if you only smell it neat.

</Callout>

### Notable perfumes featuring Sandalore

Several well-known fragrances showcase Sandalore's sandalwood profile:

- **Guerlain Samsara (1989)**: often cited as a landmark sandalwood fragrance, with Sandalore contributing to its dense, creamy woody base and long-lasting trail.
- **Chanel Égoïste (1990)**: uses Sandalore to support a bold, structured sandalwood accord that defines the fragrance's character and underpins its dramatic, memorable advertising.
- **Le Labo Santal 33 (2011)**: part of a later wave of sandalwood-centric launches, where Sandalore and related synthetics help create the smooth, modern sandalwood aura that made the scent widely recognizable.

These examples illustrate how Sandalore can adapt across decades and styles, from rich orientals to clean, contemporary woods.

## Formulation tips

Sandalore is technically forgiving and works across a broad range of product types and bases.

Practical formulation notes:

- **Application types**: suitable for fine fragrance, body sprays, lotions, haircare, and many household products where woody warmth or sandalwood nuance is desired.
- **pH stability**: stable in formulations roughly between **pH 4 and pH 9**, covering most cosmetic and toiletry applications.
- **Heat and light stability**: shows good stability under standard storage and use conditions; does not readily discolor or degrade.
- **Aging behavior**: maturation tends to **deepen and mellow** the sandalwood effect rather than producing harsh or off-notes.

Storage and shelf life:

- **Shelf life**: at least **24 months**, and often **5 years or more** when stored in a cool, dark place in tightly sealed containers.
- Use amber glass or suitable barrier packaging and minimize oxygen exposure to keep the profile fresh.

Because of its tendency to induce olfactory fatigue, rely on structured evaluation:

- Compare fresh vs. aged trials.
- Evaluate alongside other sandalwood materials.
- Use multiple evaluators when deciding dosage adjustments.

### Analytical profile (GC–MS)

Quality control for Sandalore typically relies on **gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC-MS)** analysis of each batch.

Manufacturers and perfumers use this analytical profile to:

- Confirm the identity of Sandalore and verify that overall purity is typically greater than 95%.
- Check that the ratio of its constituent stereoisomers falls within the specified range, as this blend influences the final odor profile.
- Detect and quantify any synthesis-related impurities, such as unreacted precursors or by-products, that could introduce off-odors.

The full chromatogram and detailed GC-MS data are usually treated as proprietary quality control documents and are not published with retail or catalog specifications.

## Safety and regulations

Sandalore has been well studied toxicologically and is widely used within current regulatory limits.

### Allergen and sensitization status

According to available data:

- Sandalore is **not one of the EU 26 fragrance allergens** that require labeling on cosmetic products.
- RIFM assessments describe Sandalore as **non-sensitizing** with a **low potential for sensitization** under normal conditions of use.

Always check the latest RIFM and regulatory assessments for updates, especially for leave-on cosmetics and products intended for sensitive populations.

### IFRA limits

IFRA places concentration limits on Sandalore depending on product category.

Example maximum levels under **IFRA 51st Amendment** include:

- **Category 1** (for example, lip products): **0.21%**
- **Category 4** (fine fragrance): **1.2%**
- **Category 5A**: **0.29%**
- **Category 5C**: **0.29%**
- **Category 9**: **2.3%**
- **Category 11B**: **4.5%**

<Callout kind="alert">

Use these IFRA values only as **illustrative examples**. Always consult the **full, current IFRA Standards and the latest IFRA Amendment** before finalizing any product formula for market, as limits and categories may change over time.

</Callout>

### Guidelines for skin-safe dilution

For practical formulation, keep the **concentration of Sandalore in the finished product at or below the IFRA limit** for that product category.

Concrete examples:

- For a **personal fine fragrance (IFRA Category 4)**, ensure that Sandalore in the final alcohol-based perfume does not exceed **1.2%** of the finished product.
- For a **body lotion (IFRA Category 5A)**, keep Sandalore at or below **0.29%** of the finished product.

When you build a concentrate, calculate backwards from these finished product limits so that, after dilution into the base, Sandalore remains within the applicable IFRA maximum.

### EU cosmetic regulations

Under EU cosmetics legislation:

- Sandalore is **not restricted in Annex III**, so there is no specific maximum concentration set there.
- It is **not a regulated EU fragrance allergen** requiring label declaration under the current list.

Nevertheless, you must still:

- Respect IFRA recommendations where applicable.
- Conduct a full cosmetic safety assessment for finished products, taking into account cumulative exposure and vulnerable users.

### Environmental classification

As noted above, Sandalore is inherently biodegradable but carries the classification:

- **H411: Toxic to aquatic life with long lasting effects.**

This has implications for bulk handling, manufacturing, and waste management, even though finished products typically contain much lower levels.

## Fun facts and trivia

Sandalore has attracted both perfumery and scientific interest, leading to several notable side stories.

Highlights include:

- **"Invisible" sandalwood**: many perfumers and hobbyists report that Sandalore seems to disappear from perception suddenly, illustrating the phenomenon of specific anosmia and strong olfactory adaptation.
- **Dermatology and hair research**: laboratory studies on OR2AT4 have made Sandalore a minor celebrity in dermatology and hair science, linking a fragrance ingredient to wound-healing and hair growth markers in vitro.
- **Arcadi Boix Camps commentary**: Arcadi Boix Camps famously wrote, *"It has a sweet, warm, strong, woody, santalaceous odor, which is immediately perceived on a blotter, and has a top note power about 50% greater than the essential oil of sandalwood."* This quote underscores how impactful Sandalore can be compared with natural sandalwood oil.
- **Chanel Égoïste in popular culture**: Sandalore's role in Égoïste connects it indirectly to one of perfumery's most famous advertising campaigns, the 1990 television commercial directed by **Jean-Paul Goude**, featuring women leaning from balconies and shouting "Égoïste!" in a dramatic, theatrical tableau.

For perfumers and enthusiasts, Sandalore is both a workhorse sandalwood tool and an example of how synthetic materials can develop unexpected roles far beyond their original purpose.
